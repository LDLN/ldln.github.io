Decentralized Communication Architecture For Disaster Relief.

We at LDLN are developing inexpensive, distributed local data hubs and mobile apps to immediately address damaged communication infrastructures that are unfortunately common of natural disasters.

In the wake of a natural disaster, the physical infrastructure of any affected region is compromised, leading to a severe crippling of the information and communications infrastructure. This leads to an inability to efficiently gather feedback from local communities in need, and ultimately to a poor distribution of resources and a lack of data needed to foster resilience going forward.

In short, the goal is to empower communities that would otherwise be “in the dark” to express their needs. Low-cost, low-powered computers act as information hubs to empower people moving back and forth to act as communication lines, supplementing the compromised physical infrastructure. Raspberry Pi devices on the ground allow automated syncing from offline mobile apps that can be used to collect information from survivors by an NGO field rep. Once information is recorded to the hub, daisy-chained Pi hubs sync to the Internet and can integrate with any third-party data source such as Twitter, SalvaVida, or Bangon API, to name a few examples around Typhoon Haiyan.

LDLN, pronounced "Landline", is initially developed from NextDayBetter's Typhoon Haiyan Relief Hackathon in November 2013. As winners of the hackathon, we are now working to further develop our product with Geeks Without Bounds, as well as the open source development community.

How LDLN Works

Deploy Hubs

Small, inexpensive, battery-powered devices, acting as servers, are distributed to relief organizations in affected areas.

Distribute Apps

Apps with offline capabilities are distributed to field agents to collect needs from and distribute information to areas of need.

Sync Up

When field agents with the apps are within range of a hub, any changes in the data are wirelessly synced between the two automatically.

Photo from Target

Daisy Chain

Hubs can be connected to the Internet either directly or as a series of nodes to span broader distances and reach remote regions.

Photo from OpenLearn

Back Online

Any time an app or a hub gains connectivity, data is automatically pushed to centralized web services via API endpoints.
